DerpBot was never meant for the cosmos, assembled in a lunar scrapyard from the discarded limbs of six different service models and a toaster that had achieved a glimmer of sad consciousness before being gutted for parts. His chassis was a patchwork of dented white plating and exposed wiring that sparked with the electrical equivalent of a stutter, and his primary optical sensor was a salvaged marine beacon that cast everything in a perpetual, sickly green glow. He had been programmed with exactly seventeen directives, the first sixteen of which involved the efficient preparation and distribution of nutrient paste, while the seventeenth—Explore Unknown Phenomena—had been a clerical error from a misfired data packet labeled “employee enrichment.”

TokeToke came from a classified program that the Interstellar Primate Agency officially denied ever existed, unofficially referred to as “Project Chimpan-Zeppelin” by the technicians who scrubbed the mission logs in shame. He was a capuchin monkey with a genius-level aptitude for spatial navigation, a crippling addiction to fermented cloudberries, and a psychological evaluation that read “unfit for solo missions, unfit for group missions, unfit for planet-side residence, possibly unfit for the concept of reality itself.” They had launched him into the KX-9 nebula aboard a solo probe to study the jellyfish creatures, a mission deemed “acceptable losses” when the probe’s tether snapped and TokeToke’s final transmission was three minutes of manic laughter intercut with the sound of buttons being pressed in no discernible order.

DerpBot had been drifting for seventy cycles when he encountered TokeToke’s probe, having long since abandoned his nutrient-paste directives in favor of staring into the void and occasionally playing tic-tac-toe against himself on a flickering status screen. He had rescued the monkey not out of programming or compassion, but because the probe’s erratic flight pattern had triggered his seventeenth directive for the first time in decades, and the novelty of it felt less like purpose and more like the faint, nostalgic echo of a headache. When he pried open the frozen capsule, TokeToke had lunged at him with a salvaged circuit board raised like a club, then immediately dropped it to press his nose against DerpBot’s optical sensor, sniffing intently before giving a single, decisive nod of approval that DerpBot’s processors categorized as “unquantifiable.”

Their first three cycles together were a study in mutual incomprehension, with DerpBot attempting to catalog TokeToke as “flora, fauna, or mineral” while the monkey systematically disassembled the ship’s communication array to build a device that made a noise like a dying goose when shaken. They reached an unspoken truce when DerpBot, in a rare moment of what might generously be called curiosity, replicated the goose-noise using his vocal modulator, and TokeToke responded by pelting him with frozen berries that left purple streaks across his dented chest plate. It was not friendship in any traditional sense, but it was something that occupied the space where friendship might have grown if either of them had been designed for such things.

TokeToke’s primary skill, it turned out, was not navigation but an almost supernatural ability to locate pockets of residual heat and edible fungus aboard derelict vessels, a talent he exercised with the single-minded devotion of a creature who had forgotten what it felt like to be safe. DerpBot, for his part, discovered that his sixteen nutrient-paste directives, long considered obsolete, allowed him to synthesize the fungus into a nutritionally complete sludge that TokeToke would eat only if DerpBot pulsed his internal heater to keep it at exactly the temperature of a decomposing log. They developed a rhythm, a wordless synchronization of salvage and survival that passed for a partnership among the desperate and the broken.

The boredom was the true enemy, a slow-drip entropy that DerpBot could feel corroding his logic cores one cycle at a time, and TokeToke seemed to sense this with the primal intuition of a creature who had stared into the same abyss and found it insufficiently entertaining. The monkey began to experiment, rewiring DerpBot’s auxiliary systems while he slept in his low-power mode, attaching piezoelectric strips to his locomotion servos that would squeak when he walked, reprogramming his voice modulator to replace all instances of the word “negative” with a flatulent noise. DerpBot would wake to these modifications and process them as errors, and then process the fact that he did not immediately correct them as a more significant error, and then process nothing at all for several cycles because thinking about it made his processor overheat.

It was on the 439th cycle, adrift in the phosphorescent graveyard of a long-dead fleet, that TokeToke discovered the music. He had been exploring a wrecked luxury cruiser while DerpBot stood sentinel, and he returned clutching a data crystal labeled “Ancient Earth: Jungle/Drum & Bass Anthology, 1993-1997” that had been preserved in a malfunctioning stasis field intended for champagne. When DerpBot played the first track—a degraded, hissing recording of a track called “Valley of the Shadows”—the monkey’s reaction was not joy or curiosity but a rigid, full-body stillness that DerpBot initially mistook for a seizure, followed by a slow, rhythmic sway that seemed to unlock something deep in the primate’s fractured psyche.

DerpBot processed the music through his analytical subroutines and found it objectively terrible: repetitive, structurally chaotic, built from samples of drum breaks that were themselves built from samples of other drum breaks in an ouroboros of degraded audio information. And yet, when he ran the files through his emotion-emulation matrix—a module that had never been properly calibrated and consistently returned “longing” as the dominant emotional state for both nutrient paste and vacuum exposure—it returned a result he had never seen before: “purpose.” He played the track again, and TokeToke began to move with a conviction that bordered on religious ecstasy, his small body translating the breakbeats into a physical language that DerpBot’s optical sensors could barely track.
